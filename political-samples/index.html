Sample deliverables

This is what the work can look like.
Four sample deliverables that show how Claw Forge Political Intelligence turns an issue into usable political communication assets.
Not abstract promises. Structured outputs.

These are example formats meant to show shape, standard, and usefulness. They can be adapted to campaign, commentary, civic, advocacy, and public-interest communication contexts.

Sample 1

Speech breakdown

Scenario: A public official gives a forceful speech about national direction, accountability, and reform, but leaves major implementation questions unanswered.

Main finding: The speech projects certainty and urgency, but leans heavily on tone and positioning while offering limited operational detail on implementation, benchmarks, or accountability.

Explicit claims identified:

  • the country is facing a serious governance problem
  • the speaker’s side is prepared to act decisively
  • past leadership failed to deliver real change
  • public trust can be restored through stronger direction

Weaknesses / gaps:

  • no clear implementation timeline
  • limited explanation of how results will be measured
  • heavy criticism of opponents, lighter detail on own execution plan
  • high emotional framing, lower policy specificity

Best follow-up questions:

  • What are the first 3 actions and when do they happen?
  • How will the public measure whether the promised reforms are real?
  • What accountability mechanism applies if those results are not achieved?
Sample 2

Radio-ready response script

Opening:

“Let’s strip the performance away and deal with the substance. Because yes, the speech was forceful. Yes, the tone was confident. But confidence is not a plan, volume is not evidence, and the public has every right to ask what exactly gets done, when it gets done, and who is accountable if it fails.”

Middle:

“What we heard was a message built around urgency, control, and frustration with the status quo. Fine. That may connect emotionally. But the serious question is this: where is the implementation detail? Where is the measurable roadmap? Where is the hard proof that this is more than a sharpened performance designed to sound decisive in a frustrated environment?”

“If you want public trust, then give the public more than slogans. Give timelines. Give benchmarks. Give names, mechanisms, and consequences. That is how credibility is built.”

Close:

“So the real test is not whether the speech sounded strong. The real test is whether the public can trace that strength into action, evidence, and accountability. And until that is clear, people are right to keep asking harder questions.”

Sample 3

Social response pack

Post 1: Strong tone is easy. Clear accountability is harder. The public deserves more than performance.

Post 2: If the claim is serious, the evidence should be serious too. Where is the timeline? Where are the measurable steps?

Post 3: Big speeches can move emotion. Real leadership must also move structure, results, and public trust.

Post 4: The question is not “did it sound powerful?” The question is “can the public verify what follows?”

Sample 4

Public brief

What happened: A public speech framed the national moment as urgent and called for stronger action.

Why it matters: Public speeches shape perception, trust, and pressure on institutions. If the language is strong but the details are weak, confusion increases even when attention rises.

What the public should watch next:

  • whether clear actions are announced
  • whether measurable goals appear
  • whether accountability is specific or vague
  • whether the rhetoric is matched by implementation
